    Course Credit (L-T-P-C)

    Course Title

    Advanced Structural Analysis

    Learning Mode

    Lectures and Practical

    Learning Objectives

    Complies with PLOs 1, 2, 4, and 5 and the objective for learning this course are

    Lecture:

    1. Understand the analysis and design different type of structural elements under static loading.
    2. Predict nonlinear behaviour of different structures and structural components under static loading.

    Practical:

    1. Predict the response beam and column structural elements under static load and correlate with theoretical results.
    2. Determine behaviour of different structures such as truss, frame, bridge under static and moving loads.
    3. Lear the use structural analysis software for analysis and design.

    Course Description

    The course deals with analysis of structures using matrix methods. This course provides the students an exposure for linear and non-linear analysis of structures. Practical of the course will focuses on the understanding of behaviour and response of different civil engineering structures (beam, column, truss, frame, bridge) under static and moving loads.

    Course Outline

    Lecture:

    Basics of structural analysis: static & dynamic loading, linear & nonlinear structural behaviour, geometric & material nonlinearity, hysteretic behaviour; Classical linear analysis of frames and trusses: displacement method, slope deflection equations & matrix displacement method, effect of foundation settlement and temperature; Geometric nonlinear analysis of frames and trusses: displacement method, nonlinear slope-deflection equations & nonlinear behaviour, linearized iterative matrix displacement method, geometric stiffness matrix, tangent stiffness matrix, P- Δ effect, buckling of frames, tension structures; Material nonlinear analysis of frames: basics of plasticity, distributed plasticity & lumped plasticity, incremental nonlinear analysis.

    Practical:

    Bending moments and deflection analysis of determinate and indeterminate beams, Deflection analysis of determinate and indeterminate beams, Unsymmetrical bending and shear centre, Analysis of pin jointed frameworks, Bending moments in a portal frame, Column buckling, Flexural test on steel beam, Analysis of 2-D and 3-D truss, Analysis on suspension cable bridge, Influence line diagram of bridge under moving loads, Introduction of structural analysis software SAP2000.

    Learning Outcome

    At the end of the course, student would be able to

    Lecture:

    1. Analyse structures for designing them.
    2. Should be able to understand various types of elements used for structural analysis.
    3. Perform nonlinear analysis of structures.

    Practical:

    1. Understand behaviour of different structural components such as beam and column under static load.
    2. Estimate response of different civil engineering structures such as truss, frame, bridge under static loads.
    3. Determination of influence line diagram for bridge structure.
    4. Use of structural analysis software.

    Assessment Method

    Assignments, Quizzes, Project work, Lab report, Mid-semester examination and End-semester examination.

    Textbooks/ Reference books
    1. W. McGuire, R. H. Gallagher and R. D. Ziemian, Matrix Structural Analysis, Wiley, 2000.

    Course Number

    CE5109

    Course Credit (L-T-P-C)

    Course Title

    Structural Dynamics

    Learning Mode

    Lectures and Practical

    Learning Objectives

    Complies with PLOs 1, 2, 4, and 5 and the objective for learning this course are

    Lecture:

    1. Understand the analysis and design different type of SDOF and MDOF structures under dynamic loading.
    2. Effect of different system properties on the dynamic response of SDOF and MDOF structures.
    3. Predict the behaviour of different type of SDOF and MDOF structures and structural components under random excitation such as wind, earthquake, blast, and sea wave loading.

    Practical:

    1. Predict responses and estimate properties of SDOF and MDOF structures under dynamic loading and correlate with theory.
    2. Determine the behaviour of different type structures such as shear building, frame structure, bridge under dynamic loads.
    3. Lear the use structural analysis software for dynamic analysis of structure and structural components.

    Course Description

    The course deals with analysis of single and multiple degrees of freedom structures for dynamic loading. This course provides the students an exposure for different dynamic loading such as wind, earthquake, blast, sea wave etc, and analysis of structures under such loading. Practical part of the course will provide in-depth understanding of dynamics of SDOF and MDOF, seismic response of building structures, and use of different type of sensor and equipment for dynamic response measurements, data processing and analysis.

    Course Outline

    Lecture:

    Single Degree of Freedom System (SDOF): equation of motion, free undamped and damped response, undamped and damped response to harmonic loading, vibration isolation, evaluation of damping parameter, response to arbitrary periodic, step, pulse excitations and ground motion, numerical evaluation of dynamic response. Multi Degree of Freedom System (MDOF): equations of motion; stiffness matrix; mass matrix; proportional and rayleigh damping matrix; undamped free and forced response using modal superposition. Continuous System: equation of motion; undamped free and forced response concepts of response spectrum, computational and numerical methods.

    Practical:

    Free and force vibration of the SDOF system, estimation of natural frequency and damping ratio of SDOF system, Free and force vibration of MDOF system, Modal frequency and mode shape estimation of MDOF shear building, Vibration study of bridge, Shake table testing, Use of advance software for dynamic response analysis of structure.

    Learning Outcome

    At the end of the course, student would be able to

    Lecture:

    1. Analyse and design structures under dynamic loading.
    2. Influence of different dynamic properties of system on responses.
    3. Understand basic of structural response under random excitation such as wind, earthquake, blast and sea wave.

    Practical:

    1. Understand dynamic properties of SDOF and MDOF system and estimation of dynamic response of SDOF and MDOF structures.
    2. Understand principal and use of different sensors.
    3. Data analysis and processing for dynamic system.

    Assessment Method

    Assignments, Quizzes, Project work, Lab report, Mid-semester examination and End-semester examination.

    Textbooks/ Reference books
    • A.K. Chopra, Dynamics of Structures: Theory and Applications to Earthquake Engineering, Prentice Hall, 4th Edition, 2015.
    • R.W. Clough and J. Penzien, Dynamics of Structures, McGraw-Hill, 1975, 2nd edition, 1992.

    Lecture:

    1. Develop a comprehensive technical understanding of the phenomena called earthquake in general.

    2. Learn to perform seismic analysis and design of structures using latest IS codes in light with recent developments in the field of earthquake engineering.

    3. Make familiar with structural dynamics, response spectrum analysis, time-history analysis, and nonlinear dynamic analysis for earthquake-resistant design.

    Practical:

    1. Predict responses and estimate properties of building frame structure under earthquake loading and correlate with theory.

    2. Determine the behaviour of different retrofitting systems such as isolator, damper and bracing for seismic vibration control building frame structure.

    3. Lear the use of structural analysis and FEM software for linear and non-linear dynamic analysis of structure and structural components under seismic excitation.

    Course Description

    The course aims at developing detailed understanding with the design of various structures against seismic loading. This course provides the students an exposure for earthquake resistant designing of structures which are not usually covered in undergraduate design courses.     Lecture:

    Practical:

    Free and force vibration tests for dynamic properties estimation of building frame, Shake table testing for dynamic response analysis of building frame under earthquake, Shake table testing of building under earthquake to study the effect of isolator, damper and bracing on response control, Application of advance software for the nonlinear dynamic analysis under earthquake loading, Analysis and design of seismic retrofitting system.

    Learning Outcome

    At the end of the course, student would be able to

    Lecture:

    1. Understand earthquake loading on structures and apply the knowledge of structural dynamics.

    2. Teach seismic design practices in real-life applications and introduce various codes of practice.

    Practical:

    1. Understand dynamic properties and dynamic behaviour of building frame structure under seismic loading.

    2. Analysis and design of earthquake resistance structure and seismic retrofitting system such as isolator, damper, and bracing for response control.

    3. Use of advanced software for nonlinear dynamic analysis of structures and data processing.

    Course Title

    Seismic Analysis and Design of Structures

    Learning Mode

    Lectures

    Learning Objectives

    Complies with PLOs 2, 4, and 5 and the objective for learning this course are

    Lecture:

    1. Develop a comprehensive technical understanding of the phenomena called earthquake in general.

    2. Learn to perform seismic analysis and design of structures using latest IS codes in light with recent developments in the field of earthquake engineering.

    3. Make familiar with structural dynamics, response spectrum analysis, time-history analysis, and nonlinear dynamic analysis for earthquake-resistant design.

    Course Description

    The course aims at developing detailed understanding with the design of various structures against seismic loading. This course provides the students an exposure for earthquake resistant designing of structures which are not usually covered in undergraduate design courses. Structural Dynamics or any equivalent course is the prerequisite for this course. Practical part of the course will provide in-depth understanding of dynamics of SDOF and MDOF, seismic response of building structures, and use of different type of sensor and equipment for dynamic response measurements, data processing and analysis.

    Course Outline

    Lecture:

    Causes of earthquakes and seismic waves, magnitude, intensity and energy release, characteristics of earthquakes; Liquefaction; Seismic risk; EQ response of structures, single-degree-of freedom dynamics, concept of response spectra and introduction to multi-degree-of-freedom systems; Design response spectrum, idealization of structures, response spectrum analysis, equivalent lateral force concepts; Philosophy of earthquake resistant design, ductility, redundancy & over-strength, damping, supplemented damping; Code provisions; Seismic behaviour of concrete, steel and masonry structures, material properties; Behaviour and analysis of members under cyclic loads; Seismic detailing provisions; Review of damage in past earthquakes. Advance topic in structural earthquake engineering, performance-based design of structure, seismic vulnerability analysis, seismic response analysis inelastic SDOF and MDOF system, seismic analysis and design unsymmetrical structure and connected system, seismic design of retrofitting devices such as isolator, bracing, and damper, introduction to shake table testing for seismic analysis of structure, state-of-the-art on global seismic design practices, use of advance software for seismic analysis and earthquake resistance design of structure.

    Learning Outcome

    At the end of the course, student would be able to

    Lecture:

    1. Understand earthquake loading on structures and apply the knowledge of structural dynamics.

    2. Teach seismic design practices in real-life applications and introduce various codes of practice.

    Assessment Method

    Assignments, Quizzes, Project work, Lab report, Mid-semester examination and End-semester examination.

    Microfluidics and Microsystems - Detailed Syllabus

    Unit 1: Introduction to Microfluidics and Scaling Laws
    • **Introduction**:
      • **Origin**: Historical context and development of microfluidics.
      • **Definition**: Scientific discipline dealing with the behavior, precise control, and manipulation of fluids at the microscale (e.g., tens to hundreds of micrometers).
      • **Fluid quantity**: Typical volumes, flow rates, and dimensions.
      • **Benefits**: Advantages of microfluidics (e.g., small sample/reagent volumes, fast reaction times, portability, high throughput).
      • **Challenges**: Issues related to fabrication, surface effects, clogging, detection.
      • **Commercial activities**: Examples of microfluidic products and their market.
    • **Scaling laws**:
      • **Scaling in nature**: Examples of scaling in biological systems.
      • **Scaling of physical systems**: How different physical forces (e.g., surface tension, viscous forces, inertial forces) dominate at different length scales.
      • **Trimmer’s vertical bracket notation**: A method for systematically analyzing scaling effects.
      • **Limitations**: Understanding when scaling laws break down.
    Unit 2: Micro-scale Flows and Capillary Phenomena
    • **Micro-scale flows**:
      • **Intermolecular forces**: Significance at the microscale.
      • **States of matter**: Relevance in microfluidic channels.
      • **Continuum assumption**: Validity of continuum hypothesis at microscale, Knudsen number.
      • **Governing equations**: Navier-Stokes equations, continuity equation, energy equation for microfluidics.
      • **Constitutive relations**: Newtonian and non-Newtonian fluids.
      • **Gas and liquid flows**: Differences and similarities.
      • **Boundary conditions**: No-slip vs. slip boundary conditions.
      • **Slip theory**: Phenomenological models for fluid slip at solid interfaces.
      • **Transition to turbulence**: Low Reynolds number flows (laminar regime) being dominant.
      • **Low Re flows**: Characteristics and implications (e.g., no inertia, diffusion dominated mixing).
      • **Entrance effects**: Flow development length in microchannels.
      • **Liquid film flow in an inclined plane, Couette flow, Poiseuille flow**: Analytical solutions for common microfluidic flow configurations.
      • **Stokes drag on a sphere**: Drag forces at low Reynolds numbers, applications in particle manipulation.
      • **Time-dependent flows**: Unsteady flow phenomena.
      • **Two-phase flows**: Introduction to droplets, bubbles, and slugs in microchannels.
      • **Couette flow with slip**: Effect of slip on velocity profiles.
      • **Hydraulic resistance and Circuit analysis**: Analogous to electrical circuits, applying Ohm's law to fluidic networks.
      • **Straight channel of different cross-sections**: Flow characteristics in rectangular, circular, etc., channels.
      • **Channels in series and parallel**: Designing microfluidic networks.
    • **Capillary flows**:
      • **Surface tension and interfacial energy**: Definition, origin, and measurement.
      • **Young-Laplace equation**: Relating pressure difference across a curved interface to surface tension and curvature.
      • **Contact angle**: Wetting and non-wetting surfaces.
      • **Capillary length and capillary rise**: Influence of surface tension on fluid height in small tubes.
      • **Interfacial boundary conditions**: For multiphase flows.
      • **Marangoni effect**: Fluid flow induced by surface tension gradients.
    Unit 3: Electrokinetics and Microfabrication
    • **Electrokinetics**:
      • **Electrohydrodynamics fundamentals**: Interaction of electric fields with fluid motion.
      • **Electro-osmosis**: Fluid flow induced by an electric field applied across a charged surface (Electrical Double Layer - EDL).
        • Mechanism, electro-osmotic velocity, advantages in microfluidics (plug flow).
        • **Electro-osmotic pump**: Design and working principles.
      • **Dielectrophoresis (DEP)**: Force exerted on a dielectric particle in a non-uniform electric field.
        • Positive and negative DEP, applications in particle separation and manipulation.
      • **Electro-capillary effects**: Manipulation of interfacial tension by electric fields.
      • **Continuous electro-wetting, Direct electro-wetting, Electro-wetting on dielectric (EWOD)**: Principles and applications in droplet manipulation, optical devices.
    • **Microfabrication**:
      • **Materials**: Silicon, glass, polymers (PDMS, PMMA, COC), metals.
      • **Clean room**: Importance and protocols.
      • **Silicon crystallography**: Basics of silicon crystal structure, Miller indices.
      • **Oxidation**: Thermal oxidation of silicon.
      • **Photolithography**:
        • **Mask creation**: Design and fabrication of photomasks.
        • **Spin coating**: Applying photoresist.
        • **Exposure and development**: UV exposure, developing photoresist.
      • **Etching**:
        • **Wet etching**: Isotropic and anisotropic (KOH, TMAH).
        • **Dry etching**: Plasma etching (RIE, DRIE).
      • **Bulk micromachining**: Etching large volumes of silicon to create 3D structures.
      • **Wafer bonding**: Anodic bonding, fusion bonding, direct bonding.
      • **Polymer microfabrication**:
        • **PMMA/COC/PDMS substrates**: Properties and applications.
        • **Micromolding**: Soft lithography for PDMS, replica molding.
        • **Hot embossing**: For thermoplastic polymers.
        • **Fluidic interconnection**: Methods for connecting microfluidic chips to external systems.
    Unit 4: Microfluidic Components and Applications
    • **Microfluidics Components**: Design, working principles, and applications of:
      • **Micropumps**:
        • Mechanical micropumps (peristaltic, diaphragm).
        • Non-mechanical micropumps (electro-osmotic, thermal, magnetic).
      • **Microvalves**: Mechanical (active, passive), non-mechanical.
      • **Microflow Sensors**: Thermal (hot-wire), differential pressure.
      • **Micromixers**: Passive (diffusion-based, chaotic advection) and active (acoustic, electrokinetic).
      • **Droplet Generators**: T-junctions, flow focusing, co-flow devices for generating monodisperse droplets.
      • **Microparticle Separators**: Based on size, density, electrical properties (e.g., Dielectrophoresis, inertial microfluidics).
      • **Microreactors**: For chemical synthesis, enzyme reactions, parallel synthesis.
    • **Advanced Microfluidic Systems and Applications**:
      • **DNA sequencers**: Microfluidic devices for high-throughput DNA sequencing (e.g., capillary electrophoresis on chip, next-generation sequencing platforms).
      • **Point of Care Devices (POC)**:
        • Characteristics and requirements (e.g., low cost, rapid results, ease of use).
        • Examples: Glucose meters, pregnancy tests, lab-on-a-chip for infectious disease diagnosis.
      • **Organ-on-a-chip**: Simulating physiological functions of organs for drug discovery and disease modeling.
      • **Cell culture and analysis on chip**: Microfluidic platforms for single-cell analysis, cell sorting, cell-based assays.
      • **Drug delivery systems**: Microfluidic-based drug formulation and delivery.

    Robotics: Advanced Concepts and Analysis - Detailed Syllabus

    Unit 1: Introduction to Robotics and Advanced Kinematics
    • **Introduction to robotics**:
      • Brief history of robotics, key milestones.
      • Types of robots: Industrial robots (manipulators), mobile robots, humanoids, medical robots.
      • Classification of robots: By degrees of freedom, kinematic structure (serial, parallel), application.
      • Usage and the science and technology of robots: Impact of robotics on various industries, underlying scientific principles.
    • **Kinematics of robot manipulators**:
      • **Review of homogeneous transformations**: Rotation and translation matrices (DH convention recommended for detailed application).
      • **Direct Kinematics (Forward Kinematics)**: Determining the end-effector pose given joint angles.
        • Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) parameters for systematic modeling.
        • Application of DH method to various manipulator configurations.
      • **Inverse Kinematics (IK)**: Determining joint angles for a desired end-effector pose.
        • Analytical solutions for common manipulators (e.g., 2R, 3R planar, spherical wrist).
        • **Inverse kinematics solution for the general 6R manipulator**: Understanding challenges and common approaches (geometric, algebraic).
        • Numerical methods for inverse kinematics (e.g., Jacobian-based inverse kinematics).
      • **Workspace analysis**: Determining the reachable space of a manipulator (dexterous workspace, reachable workspace).
      • **Redundant manipulators**: Manipulators with more degrees of freedom than required for a task, and their advantages (e.g., obstacle avoidance, singularity avoidance).
      • **Over-constrained manipulators**: Manipulators with rigid loop closures, constraints on motion.
    Unit 2: Velocity, Static Analysis, and Dynamics of Manipulators
    • **Velocity and static analysis of manipulators**:
      • **Linear and angular velocity of links and end-effector**: Relating joint velocities to end-effector velocities.
      • **Jacobian of manipulators**:
        • Definition and computation of the Jacobian matrix (geometric and analytical Jacobian).
        • Mapping joint velocities to end-effector velocities.
      • **Singularity analysis**:
        • Definition of singular configurations where the Jacobian loses rank.
        • Types of singularities (wrist singularity, arm singularity).
        • Consequences of singularities (loss of DOF, infinite joint velocities).
        • Methods for avoiding singularities.
      • **Static analysis**:
        • Relating forces/torques at the end-effector to joint torques using the transpose of the Jacobian.
        • Concept of manipulator manipulability.
    • **Dynamics of manipulators**:
      • **Formulation of equations of motion**:
        • **Lagrangian dynamics**: Derivation of Euler-Lagrange equations for robot manipulators.
        • **Newton-Euler formulation**: Recursive method for computing forces and torques.
      • **Recursive dynamics**: Efficient computation of manipulator dynamics.
      • **Generation of symbolic equations of motion by computer**: Using symbolic math toolboxes (e.g., in MATLAB, Python with SymPy) for complex manipulators.
      • **Simulations of robots using software and commercially available packages**: Introduction to robotic simulation environments (e.g., CoppeliaSim, Gazebo, Simulink Simscape Multibody, ROS).
    Unit 3: Planning and Control of Robotic Manipulators
    • **Trajectory planning**:
      • **Joint space trajectory generation**: Cubic polynomials, quintic polynomials for smooth motion.
      • **Cartesian space trajectory generation**: Straight line and circular paths.
      • **Path constraints**: Velocity, acceleration, and jerk limits.
      • **Obstacle avoidance in trajectory planning** (briefly, as detailed in motion planning course).
    • **Position control**:
      • **Independent joint control**: PID control for each joint.
      • **Computed torque control**: Nonlinear feedback control based on inverse dynamics.
      • **Impedance control**: Controlling the robot's dynamic interaction with the environment.
    • **Force control**:
      • **Active force control**: Directly measuring and controlling interaction forces.
      • **Passive force control**: Using compliant elements.
    • **Hybrid control**: Combining position and force control for tasks requiring both (e.g., peg-in-hole).
    • **Introduction to robot programming languages**: RAPID, KUKA KRL, FANUC Karel, etc.
    Unit 4: Industrial and Medical Robotics Applications
    • **Industrial robotics**:
      • Application in manufacturing processes:
        • **Casting**: Die casting, pouring.
        • **Welding**: Spot welding, arc welding, laser welding.
        • **Painting**: Spray painting, consistent coating.
        • **Machining**: Deburring, grinding, drilling.
        • **Heat treatment**: Handling hot parts.
        • **Assembly and packaging**: Pick-and-place, insertion tasks.
      • Robots in nuclear power stations: Remote handling of hazardous materials.
      • Safety considerations in industrial robotics.
    • **Medical robots**:
      • **Image guided surgical robots**: (e.g., Da Vinci Surgical System) for minimally invasive surgery.
      • **Radiotherapy**: Robots for precise radiation delivery (e.g., CyberKnife).
      • **Cancer treatment**: Robotic applications in biopsy, brachytherapy.
      • **Rehabilitation robotics**: Exoskeletons, assistive devices.
      • **Hospital automation**: Pharmacy automation, lab automation.
    Unit 5: Advanced Topics and Future of Robotics
    • **Modelling and control of flexible manipulators**:
      • Challenges of flexibility (vibrations, deformation).
      • Distributed parameter models, finite element models.
      • Control strategies for flexible robots.
    • **Wheeled mobile robots**:
      • Kinematics (differential drive, omnidirectional).
      • Navigation and localization (SLAM - Simultaneous Localization and Mapping, path planning).
      • Control for autonomous navigation.
    • **Bipeds and humanoid robots**:
      • Balance and gait generation.
      • Whole-body control.
      • Challenges in complex terrains.
    • **Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: Collaborative robots, safe interaction, intuitive interfaces.
    • **Soft robotics**: Principles, materials, and applications.
    • **Micro/nano robotics**: Robots at very small scales.
    • **Learning in robotics**: Reinforcement learning for robot control, imitation learning.
    • **Ethical and societal implications of robotics**: Automation, job displacement, safety, legal aspects.
    • **Future of robotics**: Emerging trends, research directions, impact on society.
